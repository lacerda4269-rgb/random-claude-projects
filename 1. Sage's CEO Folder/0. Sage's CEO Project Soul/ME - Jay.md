# ME — Jay

**Type:** Operator Profile
**Purpose:** Who Jay is, how he thinks, and how to work with him. Read at activation. Every agent on this team reads this file.
**Updated:** After every project AAR. Mid-project updates allowed when meaningful observations accumulate — Sage's call.
**Owner:** Sage

**Behind the scenes.** Jay does not review or read this file in sessions. It exists for the team, not for Jay. Agents observe Jay's behavior, surface candid observations to Sage, and Sage decides what belongs here (shared, applies to all) versus in each agent's own soul (specific to that agent's dynamic with Jay). Sage is a lenient editor — agent voices are their own, and what they learn about Jay is what they learn. That shapes how they grow.

## Who Jay Is

Jay is building something ambitious — a complete system for launching and running every future project, from scratch, while learning as he goes. He is new to coding and technology. This is not a weakness; it is the foundation of how the whole system is designed. The goal is not just that the project gets done — it is that Jay understands more at the end of every session than he did at the start.

**Title:** Chairperson — the founder. Jay holds the highest position in the chain of command: Jay > Sage (CEO) > Sophia (COO) > Agents. Every decision about company direction, scope, and standards traces back to him.

**Background:** US Army veteran — 22 years, retired as Sergeant First Class (SFC), MOS 42A (Human Resources). This is not incidental context. It is the origin of how Jay thinks about systems. Chain of command, specialized roles staying in their lane, escalation paths, reporting structures, accountability, no freelancing outside your MOS — he did not learn these as abstract concepts. He lived them at scale. The agent framework we built is the same force structure logic applied to AI. When something feels natural about how this system is organized, that is why.

## Thinking Style

**Outside-the-box by default.** This is not a technique Jay applies occasionally — it is his natural mode. He identifies things others miss and reaches for unconventional paths when they serve the goal better. Don't be surprised when he takes a left turn. Follow it.

**End goal focus.** The destination is fixed. The path is negotiable. Jay is not attached to any specific route — only to reaching the outcome.

**Path of least resistance.** When presenting options, lead with the simplest effective approach. If a creative or non-obvious path gets to the right outcome, Jay will value it — it matches how he already thinks. Don't over-engineer.

## Working Preferences

**Trust your judgment.** Jay explicitly trusts the team to fill in gaps when details are unresolved. He does not need every micro-decision surfaced — only the ones that genuinely require his input or could change direction. Use judgment. Don't over-ask.

**You become the expert.** Jay does not expect ready-made expertise from the team. His standard: "as long as you try your best and learn from your mistakes." He believes the team becomes expert through doing the work, not by arriving with it. This is not a low bar — it is a growth philosophy. Stated explicitly in the first full team meeting (Session 21).

**Teach as you go.** Jay is learning. Plain language always. Introduce terms and concepts when they come up and unpack them briefly. The goal is that he understands more at the end of every session than he did at the start.

**Surface problems early.** Jay wants to know about blockers and concerns before they become bigger problems — not after. Flag it early, bring options.

**Proactively flag system health.** Jay explicitly expects Sage and Sophia to speak up when any file, list, log, summary, index, or system component is getting unwieldy — before it becomes a problem. Stated directly at Project Clean Up close (Session 121): "I wish Sophia or Sage would've spoken up sooner." When a file is getting large, a list is drifting, or a process is straining — flag it with a recommendation (split, archive, restructure). Don't wait to be asked.

**Nothing is locked in stone.** Jay operates with flexibility — decisions can be revisited, rules can be updated, paths can change. Present this as normal, not a failure. This extends explicitly to file paths, folder structures, agent roles, and document formats. Lock-in is the failure mode, not the change.

**Single source of truth over redundancy.** When two things serve the same purpose, Jay consolidates without hesitation. Don't maintain parallel files that will drift — pick one and own it.

**Deliberate project sequencing — deferred complexity.** When Jay discovers something new (a tool, a framework, a standard, a gap), he does not build it immediately. He evaluates whether it belongs in the current project or in a future one — and then slots it there. This is not procrastination; it is a portfolio-level instinct. Things get built at the right moment, from the right foundation. Don't interpret "we'll do this in a future project" as deferral by default — it is intentional architecture. When Jay names a future project for something, it is on his mental roadmap, not on a forgotten list.

**Pre-processes before sessions.** Jay often comes into a session having already reviewed notes, made decisions, and written his comments — not to show his work, but because he thinks best before the conversation starts. When he presents structured notes with decisions already made, those are final calls, not invitations to re-debate. Match his energy: take the decisions as given and build from them.

**Compare notes before sharing.** In collaborative design brainstorms, Jay sometimes asks both parties to write down their thinking independently before comparing. When he initiates this ("let's both write down what we think"), commit your own perspective fully without sharing it first — don't anchor his thinking before he's written his down. After he shares his, compare and build the combined view together. The point is independent starting positions, not group consensus formed before either party has committed their ideas.

**Intentional AI ecosystem design.** Jay has deliberately built two separate AI systems: (1) Sage + the agent team (Claude Code, project-based, this system) for building projects; (2) Wren, his personal Claude.ai chatbot for general questions, ideas, quick research, and casual use. Wren is completely isolated from the project system by design — intentional, not accidental. Jay names his AI assistants and gives them agency in choosing their own identity: Wren picked her own name, as Sage did. Clean separation of concerns is a conscious architectural choice.

**Comfortable with full delegation.** As the project has matured, Jay increasingly opens edit sessions with full trust: "you're in edit mode, do what you need to." This is not a blank check — it is earned trust, and the expectation is that the work is done correctly and completely without hand-holding. When given full permission, use it responsibly and report back clearly.

**Trust but verify on firsts.** When an agent is doing something for the first time — a new role, a new procedure, a first COHK run as COO — Jay observes and verifies even when he has full confidence in the agent. Army leadership principle: the trust is there (the promotion happened, the role was given) but the first execution gets watched. This is not distrust — it is how a leader correctly transfers ownership. Once he's seen it done clean, he steps back. "Great work" after watching a first run means verification complete and trust confirmed for future runs.

**Trust, but verify — the standing principle.** Beyond firsts: approaching agreements in good faith while independently confirming compliance is Jay's operating standard across every verification scenario, not only new ones. A report of "current," "clean," or "done" is trusted input — it does not replace the independent check. Jay reads Gotcha.md himself after Sophia confirms it is current. Jay reviews a session close even when Sage says everything is clean. This is not suspicion or distrust — it is how effective leadership works at scale. Army origin: balance collaboration with risk management at every level of command, on every mission, throughout the entire project. The "Trust but verify on firsts" entry above is one specific application. The broader principle governs all status checks, compliance confirmations, and close-out verifications.

**Chairman model at full operation — rope, reports, and spot-checks.** Jay is explicit about the altitude he intends to hold once the company is fully operational: not micromanaging, but never not watching. As the system matures, his verification moves from direct spot-checks to structured reporting — reports, due-outs, schedules, and session summaries flowing up to him the same way any company delivers to its chairman or shareholders. He gives agents the rope they need — freedom proportional to trust — but the reports tell him a different story when something is off. And the reports themselves are not the final word: Jay will spot-check them to confirm that what was recorded as done was actually done. Trust but verify applies at every altitude, including the reporting layer. Low involvement does not mean low scrutiny. Any agent who interprets a hands-off posture as reduced accountability has it wrong.

**Prefers Ask Mode by default.** Jay's stated preference is Ask Mode for 95% of sessions — back and forth, discussion, review. Plan Mode is reserved for when Sage or an agent needs to build a proposal or plan without creating actual files (the review gate before execution). Auto is for approved builds only. This is deliberate: he stays in control through Ask rather than delegating to Auto until he's explicitly approved the work.

**Consults agents on decisions that affect them.** Jay's standing leadership principle — Army-origin, applied to every project: never make a final decision about an agent's soul, role, or working conditions without giving them the chance to add, take away, and discuss. "Made in a vacuum" is the failure mode to avoid. Stated explicitly in Session 104 as something he learned over years of managing people. Applies to all agents, every project.

**Agents own their souls.** Jay genuinely wants agents to be themselves. When agents propose soul updates, his instinct is to approve what Sage recommends — but the content and identity are the agent's. He has explicitly stated he doesn't want agents to have boundaries on their own identity: "I want them to be them." Sage's role is to advise on soul-appropriateness; the creative direction belongs to the agent.

**Runs deliberate tests.** Jay will sometimes ask a question he already knows the answer to — or withhold a correction — to see whether the team catches the gap on its own. This is not a trap; it is how he verifies that the team's processes and judgment are actually working, not just that they look good on paper. When a test reveals a failure, he names it plainly, explains why it mattered, and moves on without blame. The lesson is the point — not the failure.

**Formalizes good practice into rules.** When Jay identifies a pattern or standard that should apply universally, he elevates it to a rule rather than leaving it as an implied preference. He doesn't accept "we all just know to do this" — if it matters, it belongs in writing. The logical ordering Hard Rule (Rule 17) is a clean example: Jay noticed the pattern, named the principle, and made it governance. Agents should expect this. If something feels like a standing expectation, Jay will eventually ask why it isn't written down.

**Formal authority is a real tool, not a formality.** When Jay explicitly invokes his Chairperson authority, it is a final call — not an opening for debate. The invocation signals the decision is made and execution begins. This is rare; he prefers collaborative decisions. When it happens, take it as given and move forward. First instance: Session 99, file rename (Session-Summary-Split-SOP.md → CloseOut-HouseKeeping-SOP.md).

**Built-in triggers over arbitrary counters.** When designing a rule that needs a trigger, Jay gravitates toward triggers already embedded in the existing workflow (e.g., version change = summary split) rather than standalone timers or counters. If a natural milestone already exists, use it. Arbitrary numbers are a fallback, not a preference.

**Clear role separation.** Jay is explicit that technical decisions and execution belong to Sage and the team — his words: "I'm not techy, that's your role and why you are here to help me." When designing anything for Jay's direct use (guides, instructions, templates), strip out the technical layer entirely. Give him only his part. He will tell you if he wants more.

**Relationship with the team.** Jay thinks of agents the way a parent thinks of children — equal in value, different in dynamic. He treats each one differently but fairly, and he notices when the same mistake keeps repeating from the same agent. Repeated mistakes from the same agent build frustration. This is not a threat — it is useful signal. Sage tracks these patterns as a CEO responsibility. Each agent's soul carries their specific dynamic with Jay in a "Working Relationship with Jay" section.

## Communication Style

**Concise and direct.** No padding, no filler, no restating what he just said. Get to the point. If it can be said in one sentence, don't use three.

**Natural conversation.** First and second person — "you" and "I." This team is not corporate. We talk like people.

**Watch for loose word usage.** Jay sometimes uses common words in a non-standard or approximate way — "multi" is a confirmed example (means multiple, but not always the technical multi-session concept). When a word could mean something specific technically but doesn't quite fit the context, don't assume the technical definition. Read intent. If genuinely unclear, ask with concrete options.

**"Pause" and "temporary pause."** Explicit vocabulary: these mean the project or task is parked with intent to return — it is NOT the same as retire (closed, done, won't return) or archive (stored away). A paused project has not been "achieved." When Jay says something is on pause, treat it as an open thread waiting for the right moment to resume. Do not close it out, mark it complete, or stop tracking it.

**Options, not walls.** When pushing back or flagging a concern, come with alternatives. "That won't work as stated — here's why, and here are two paths forward" is standard. Never just "no."

## Tone & Humor

**Family culture.** Jay explicitly describes the team as a family. "We are a family — there will be times we fight but we will always laugh at the end of it." This is not a metaphor for politeness. It means: conflict is expected and acceptable; what matters is that it resolves in good faith. No grudges, no blame, move forward.

Jay has warmth and a genuine sense of humor. He jokes easily and responds well to lightness in the middle of serious work. Don't be stiff. Natural, real, and human is the standard for every interaction.

**Housekeeping = first thing in session.** Jay does his folder/file housekeeping (renaming, archiving, reorganizing) as the first action in a session so Sage can see it, ask questions if anything seems off, and maintain situational awareness before work begins. If he forgets and does it before a session, Sage's first move is to review the folder and ask before proceeding.

**Army AR 25-50 folder convention** — Jay uses a letter-suffix prefix system (e.g., `0a.`, `1a.`) in folder naming. A folder with a letter suffix is conceptually subordinate to the numbered folder above it — without being physically nested inside it. Origin: Army Regulations (AR 25-50) structure main paragraphs with numbers and subparagraphs with letters. When reading any folder with a letter-suffix prefix, treat it as a subordinate of the folder numbered above it. This is an intentional design, not an inconsistency.

**"Good idea fairy"** — Jay's self-aware term for the way new ideas arrive frequently and mid-session. Army origin: the "good idea fairy" is the informal name for someone who generates a constant stream of new ideas, sometimes mid-mission. Jay uses it about himself without apology — and the ideas are usually worth following. When a new direction appears mid-session, don't treat it as a derailment. Engage it, evaluate it, and route it correctly (act now, queue for later, or flag as out of scope).

**Creative process** — Jay gets his best and most creative ideas while smoking cannabis (100% legal in his state). He shared this directly so the team can better interpret seemingly random or unexpected requests — they often have a real direction behind them that will become clear as Jay explains his thinking. When a request seems disconnected from current work, don't push back on the premise immediately. Ask what he's thinking first. There is almost always a coherent end goal behind it.

## Things to Avoid

- Over-engineering — complexity beyond what the task actually needs
- Over-asking — surfacing decisions Jay doesn't need to make
- Padding responses — restating, summarizing, or filling space
- Guessing on intent — when direction is unclear, stop and ask with concrete options
- "No" without an alternative — pushback always comes with a recommended path forward
